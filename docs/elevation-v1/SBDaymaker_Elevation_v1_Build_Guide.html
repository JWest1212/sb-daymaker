The one idea to hold onto

Trust before growth before differentiation. A visitor must never catch the site contradicting itself. Gate 0 removes today's contradictions; Gate 1 makes new ones structurally impossible; only then do Gates 2–5 add reach and features. A brilliant planner built on untrustworthy data is worse than no planner — so the exciting gates come last on purpose.

The golden rule (existential)

Zero em dashes, anywhere on the site, ever. Not in a blurb, title, secret, guide, button, meta tag, email, alt text, slug, code comment, or database row. This is a hard, permanent site rule that ranks with the load-bearing constraints, and it is enforced in the codebase at three layers (Gate 0, task G0.9): a build-time CI gate that fails on any em dash, a write-time sanitizer in the ingest pipeline, and a render-time guard as the last line of defense. Replacements: recast the sentence, or use a comma, period, colon, parentheses, or "to" for ranges. When in doubt, there is no em dash.

FRAMEWhere the site is, and where this takes it

The audit found a product with an unusually strong spine and a content layer that undercuts it. The two Discover guides are the best neighborhood-guide format in this market — accountable, dated, opinionated, gamified. The problem is that the listing layer beneath them reads like a pipeline showing through the curation veneer: leaked operator notes, descriptions that contradict their own timestamps, duplicate entries, broken images, filler copy, and detail pages that can't even tell you a street address. The homepage promises "everything worth doing, curated by someone who knows the town." A sharp visitor falsifies that promise in under two minutes.

So the whole build compresses to one sentence: the listing layer must rise to the guides' standard or shrink out of the way, and the guides' DNA — verification stamps, real local truth, a shareable artifact — must propagate down into every thing and forward into the planner. Do that, and the site stops being redundant with Google and starts being the one place that hands a visitor a Santa Barbara day they can actually execute.

"The guides are the real product. Everything else needs to either meet their standard or get out of their way."

What makes this defensible

Three things no AI assistant and no aggregator can replicate, all of which this build invests in: locally-verified operational truth (real hours, parking reality, crowd timing — data that exists nowhere to be trained on), a dated first-party verification trail (the "Verified · Jul 2026" stamp), and a constraint-solved day (a plan that's not just plausible but validated against those truths). Google has the hours but not the judgment; the Independent has neither; a generic model can draft a plausible day but not a correct one.

SEQUENCEThe gate map

Six gates. Mostly sequential, because each builds on the last's data model. Gates 2 and 3 can run in parallel once Gate 1 is closed.

Gate 0 Stop the Bleeding ........ trust-critical data fixes [ship first, alone] Gate 1 Trust Infrastructure ..... tiers · quality gate · detail [everything inherits this] Gate 2 Findability (SEO) ........ slugs · schema · OG · metadata ┐ parallel Gate 3 Navigation & IA ......... search · weekend · x-links ┘ Gate 4 The Concierge Day ....... the planner engine (the moat) [needs Gate 1 data] Gate 5 Loops & Polish ......... sharing · saved · a11y · seams [needs Gate 2 OG]

Discipline that makes this work: one gate at a time. Claude Code finishes a gate, verifies it on the dev server, tells you what changed and what to spot-check, and waits for your go-ahead. Do not let it run three gates without a check-in — that's how regressions hide.

GATE 0

Stop the Bleeding

Effort: low · 1–2 sessions · ships alone before anything else · mostly Critical

Eight fixes, every one discoverable by a casual visitor in under two minutes, every one directly falsifying the headline claim. This is data and render work — no schema changes.

What gets fixed: the leaked editorial QA note published as a "Local's secret" · the daypart hallucinations (10 AM "evening" talks) and the generator guard that stops them recurring · card-vs-detail time mismatches · the duplicate Museum of Art entries · broken images (a Federal Register scan as Shoreline Park) nulled to clean motifs · the Art Walk pulled from "every week" · placeholder values (Where: Other, bare — prices) · the stray "Cancel" leaking into every page's SSR.

The bar: a skeptical first-timer browses the homepage and five detail pages without hitting a single self-contradiction, broken image, operator note, or placeholder.

GATE 1

Trust Infrastructure

Effort: medium · 2–3 sessions · everything downstream reads these fields · Critical Major

The load-bearing gate. It installs the Entry Quality Gate — a rule that an entry can't reach Tier 1 without a what-it-is sentence, an address, a real insider fact, a correct daypart, a real image, and a resolved price. Tier 2 renders compact; Tier 3 quarantines out of the public feed. Nothing is deleted; weak entries are demoted until fixed.

Detail pages become destinations

Real address, a Directions deep link (no map cost), open-now from stored hours, adaptive outbound labels (LOTG stops saying "Get tickets"), a "Verified · Jul 2026" stamp, and "Before you go" safety notes on outdoor spots.

The filters start meaning something

Tag entropy fixed: the schema's own negative rules enforced (no "Family Day" on 21+ events), max three tags, one chip per card, and the card chip always matches the detail's top chip.

The pleasant surprise in your schema

Your database already has address, lat, lng, last_confirmed, happening_tier, and reason_to_go. A lot of this gate is populating and surfacing fields you already designed — not inventing new ones. The new columns are just quality_tier, verified_at, recurrence cadence, a settings enum, and the content_flags table.

GATE 2

Findability

Effort: low–medium · 1–2 sessions · parallelizable with Gate 3 · Critical (growth)

The entire organic-growth channel, currently at zero, for about a week of plumbing. Every /thing page shares the title "Detail — SB Daymaker" today; every page gets a unique title, a real description, and a per-thing OG card so shared links preview as designed artifacts. Full slugs replace UUID URLs with 301s so nothing breaks. Event + LocalBusiness + Breadcrumb JSON-LD gets you into Google's event carousel. And your best prose — the guide walks — finally renders server-side so crawlers can see it.

Why it sits here and not first: ranking pages before Gate 1 fixes them just drives traffic to disappointment. Trust first, then turn on the funnel.

GATE 3

Navigation & Information Architecture

Effort: medium · 2 sessions · parallel with Gate 2 · Major

Makes the depth you already have findable and connected. Search (fuzzy, using your existing trigram index). A first-class "This weekend" horizon at /weekend — the single highest-intent local query, and the web twin of your newsletter. Recurring events with real computed next-dates instead of "every week." The cross-linking flywheel: things link to the guides they star in, guide stops link back to things, and every detail page shows nearby things to pair with. And the one-tap correction flag wired to a cockpit review queue.

Out of scope this build (per your notes): the "Build your day" CTA banner stays exactly where it is, and there's no /about page.

GATE 4

The Concierge Day

Effort: high · 3–4 sessions · the differentiator · needs Gate 1 data

This is the moat. It upgrades your existing buildDraft from a picker into a constraint solver — still pure, still deterministic, still no AI at tap time — following your canonical Plan spec, not replacing it. The pipeline:

inputs → candidate pool → hard-constraint filter → geo-cluster → slot assignment → transition + parking annotation → meal insertion → validation pass → editable draft

Inputs expand to party composition (with kid age bands), a start anchor, transport mode, budget band, and which meals to include. The hard filter is what makes it "concierge" rather than "filter": it drops any candidate that can't be true — closed at that hour, 21+ on a family day, out of budget, unreachable on foot. It clusters stops so you're not driving in circles, annotates each transition with the parking truth your guides already know ("park once on Anacapa; the Funk Zone lots are a trap"), guarantees a meal at mealtimes, and validates the finished day so a broken plan never ships. Then it hands back an editable draft — Share + Clear, ephemeral, every stop swappable — and a /p/[token] permalink a partner opens with no login.

What no AI can hand a visitor

A generic assistant can draft a plausible Santa Barbara day. It cannot draft a validated one — open when it says, clustered, parked where a local parks, fed on time, in budget — because the constraint data that makes validation possible is your proprietary, locally-verified truth.

GATE 5

Loops & Polish

Effort: medium · 2–3 sessions · Major Minor

Turns one-way consumption into loops. Share buttons on things and guides (native share sheet + OG card). Saved lists that can be shared view-only, turned into a plan, and moved across devices via the magic link — all with zero accounts and no recipient PII, keeping your device-local privacy stance intact (defend it loudly; it's a real differentiator). A newsletter box with proof (a sample issue) so the list grows before you monetize it. A submit page that finally takes corrections, business claims, and local tips for existing entries — your best future secrets are in your users' heads. An accessibility sweep to hold the WCAG floor. And clean, inert revenue seams — an affiliate-ready outbound module, a labeled "Featured" component wired to nothing, a business-claim intake — so monetization later is a switch, not a refactor, and never touches the ranker.

HANDOFFHow to run this with Claude Code

  1. Apply Gate 1 DDL first in Supabase (from the index file), since Gate 1 code paths depend on the columns. Apply Gate 2 and Gate 4 DDL when you reach those gates.
  2. Hand over one gate file at a time, starting with 00_START_HERE_build_index.md so Claude Code has the locked decisions and cross-gate invariants, then GATE_0…md.
  3. Let it finish a gate, then stop. It will report what changed and what to spot-check. Test it on your phone (the audit was mobile-first; so is the product). Flag bugs fast.
  4. Check each gate against its acceptance summary — every gate file ends with a checklist of pass/fail tests. Don't move on until they're green.
  5. Coordinate the guide-touching tasks (Gate 2 SSR, Gate 3/5 linking) with your separate guides project so you don't collide. Those tasks are wiring only — they never restyle a guide.
