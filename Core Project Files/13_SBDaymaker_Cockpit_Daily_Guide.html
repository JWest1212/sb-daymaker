Document 13 · The Daily Routine

Fifteen minutes,
every morning

The engine runs all night. This is the part that's yours: open the cockpit, glance at what came in, approve the good, fix or skip the rest. No code, no commands — just reading cards and pressing a few keys. This guide is the one you'll keep open until the routine is second nature.

☕ ~15 minutes a day ⌨️ Keyboard-fast 🔒 Nothing's public until you say so

The whole routine, in one breath

If you read nothing else, read this. The other sections just explain each step in more depth.

  1. Check the summary email. Each morning an email tells you how many items are waiting, how many were dropped (and why), and whether any source broke overnight. Skim it. Tap the link to open the cockpit.
  2. Clear the easy wins first. Press B to bulk-approve every "green" item at once (these came from the most trustworthy sources with confirmed times). A big chunk of the queue vanishes in one keystroke.
  3. Walk the rest with the keyboard. For each remaining card: glance at the title, blurb, time, and photo. A to approve, R to reject, or E to fix something first (including swapping the photo). ↓ moves to the next.
  4. Peek at what was dropped. Open the "Dropped tonight" panel. Usually you can ignore it — but if a great event was dropped only for a missing time, you can hunt down the time and add it back.
  5. Done. Close the tab. Whatever you approved is now live on the site; everything else waits or is gone. Tomorrow, repeat.
The one habit that keeps the site trustworthy

You're the last set of eyes before something goes public. The machine already threw out anything with a fuzzy time, but you catch the rest: a weird blurb, a wrong-looking photo, an event that doesn't fit. When in doubt, R (reject) — a missed event costs nothing; a bad one costs trust.

Step 1 — Open the cockpit

Where it lives

  • Your review screen is at your app's address ending in /admin/review (for example sbdaymaker.com/admin/review). Bookmark it.
  • If it asks you to sign in, use your founder magic-link login — the same one only you have.
  • The fastest path each day: just tap the link in your morning summary email; it drops you straight here.

What you'll see: a stack of cards (the queue), a summary bar across the top, and on the right two small panels — "Dropped tonight" and "Source health." That's the whole screen.

Step 2 — Read a card in three seconds

Every card is the same shape. You're really only checking four things: does the title make sense, does the blurb sound like us, is the time trustworthy, and is the photo right.

The trust chip on the time — your most important signal

Next to each event's start time is a small colored chip. It tells you how sure the machine is about that time, so you know how hard to look.

Green — "Deterministic start"
The time came straight from the venue's own ticketing or the official feed. Trust it. These are your bulk-approve material.
Amber — "Confirm cadence"
A recurring thing (like a monthly art walk). The time's fine; just glance that the "every month / every other week" pattern reads right.
Blue — "Evergreen"
A place, not a timed event (a beach, a bookstore). There's no start time to check — just confirm it's a place worth listing.

Under the time is a small "view source" link. If you ever doubt a time, click it — it opens the exact page the time came from, so you can see for yourself.

Why you'll never see a "red" time

There's no red chip because the machine already dropped anything with an unreliable time before it ever reached your queue. Everything you see has a time worth trusting — green and amber are just "very sure" vs. "sure." The dropped ones are in the side panel, not the main stack.

Step 3 — The four keys (this is the whole job)

Your hands barely leave the keyboard. Here's everything:

AApprove & publish. The card goes live on the site and slides away. (An "Undo" appears for a moment if you change your mind.)
RReject. The card is set aside and won't come back. Use this freely — skipping an event is cheap.
EEdit. Opens the card for small fixes — reword the blurb, change a tag, or swap the photo (next section). Press E again (or "Done") when finished.
↑ ↓Move between cards without touching the mouse.
BBulk-approve green. Publishes every green-chip card currently shown, all at once. Your biggest time-saver.
The fast path most mornings

Press B first to clear all the green items. Then ↓ through the handful that are left, pressing A or R on each. Most days that's the entire routine — well under fifteen minutes.

Step 4 — Swapping a photo (when the picture's wrong)

Each card arrives with a real photo already in place — the exact one that will appear on the live site — with a small label telling you where it came from ("Pexels," "Wikimedia," or "Google Places"). Your job here isn't to find an image; it's to swap to a better one if the default isn't quite right. Several alternatives are pre-loaded and ready.

How to pick a different photo

  1. Press E on the card (or click Edit). The image gets a gold outline and small ◂ ▸ arrows.
  2. Click the ▸ (or press the → key) to flip to the next photo option. Keep going to cycle through them — there are usually three to five, already loaded, so it's instant.
  3. Stop on the one you want. The little label updates to show its source. That's now the selected photo.
  4. Press E again (or "Done"), then A to approve — your chosen photo goes live with it.

The photo options were gathered overnight from free sources, so flipping through them costs nothing.

The rare card that still shows a placeholder

Because the engine already tries the paid source overnight, almost every card arrives with a real photo. A plain branded placeholder only shows up in two cases: the night's photo budget was used up, or no photo exists anywhere for that spot. If you really want an image on such a card, the picker offers a "Try fetching a photo" button — it attempts one paid pull for that single card. If the monthly budget still has room it'll grab one; if the budget's spent, the button tells you so and the placeholder stays until the budget resets next month. This is the only thing in your daily routine that could cost a few cents, it only happens when you click it, and it's rarely needed.

Step 5 — The "Dropped tonight" panel

On the right is a list of items the machine threw out overnight, each with a reason. Most days you can ignore it — but it's there so nothing disappears silently, and so you can rescue a gem.

What the reasons mean

  • "No start time" — the event looked good but its time was fuzzy ("around 8," "dusk," or a day with no clock time). The machine refuses to guess.
  • "Duplicate" — the same event already came in from a more trustworthy source, so the copy was dropped. Nothing lost.
  • "No title / no address / no source" — too incomplete to trust. Safe to ignore.

When to rescue one

If you spot a genuinely great event dropped only for "no start time," and you happen to know or can quickly find the real time, click its "Review manually" button. You can add the confirmed time yourself and push it into the queue. Don't go hunting for every one — just the occasional standout. Never invent a time to make a row pass. If you can't confirm it, leave it dropped.

When something looks off

The morning email says a source is "down"

One source breaking is normal — websites change. The other sources still ran. If the same source is down several days running, open Claude Code and say: "the [source name] adapter has returned zero for several nights — check if the site changed and fix the parser."

The queue is empty

Some mornings there's genuinely little new — that's fine, especially midweek. The screen shows a friendly "Queue cleared" message. Nothing to do; enjoy the short day.

You approved something by mistake

An Undo appears for a few seconds right after you approve. Missed it? You can still find the item live on the site and unpublish it — or ask Claude Code to help you set it back to "needs review."

A card's blurb is awkward

Press E and reword it in your own voice, then approve. The machine drafts; you have final say on every word that goes public.

That's the whole job

Open the cockpit, press B, walk the rest with A / R / E, glance at the dropped panel, close the tab. The engine does the finding; you do the judging. Fifteen minutes, and Santa Barbara has a fresh, trustworthy answer to "what's worth doing today."

Remember

Every promise the site makes to its readers passes through your morning click. The times are already guaranteed; you're guarding taste and trust. When unsure, reject — and keep the bar high.
