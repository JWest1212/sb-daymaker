01 — Executive Summary

The thesis on one page

SB Daymaker is a mobile-first daily companion for Santa Barbara. It answers a single human question — "what's worth doing in Santa Barbara today?" — and it answers it with the warmth and judgment of a knowing local friend rather than the cold completeness of a calendar.

The gap it fills

Every existing Santa Barbara resource is either a static calendar (Visit SB, DowntownSB, the city) or a weekly publication (the Independent, Edible SB). None is a daily-ritual app with personalization, a personal save layer, and a curated point of view. The national product that proves this model — DoStuff — deliberately avoids personalization and isn't in Santa Barbara.

The wedge

A happenings-first guide whose spine is what's actually happening around town — ranked in a three-tier cascade (discrete dated → recurring rhythms → evergreen reasons-to-go). It can't out-list Yelp/Google on "what exists," so it wins the lane they can't: compiling what's on. The Tier-3 evergreen floor solves the "empty on a quiet Tuesday" risk — SB is always there, even when the calendar is thin.

Who it's for

Both residents settling into the rhythm of the city and visitors trying to live it like a local — served by one unified experience. The audience is a barbell: affluent retirees, the large 25–54 professional/family middle, ~26k UCSB students next door, a ~36% Latino community, and ~6.5M mostly-Californian visitors (~64% day-trippers). Occasion tags let each person tune the app to what they're up to.

The shape of the business

~$45
Monthly platform cost (before photos)
Solo
Founder-operable with AI leverage
2-sided
Consumer app + admin cockpit

SB Daymaker is designed to be run by one person. An AI editorial engine drafts content (event blurbs, daily picks, persona tags) and the founder approves it through a private admin cockpit in ~15 minutes a day. The business can sustain as a beloved hobby at near-zero cost, or scale into local-sponsorship revenue — the architecture supports both without a rebuild.

The single most important strategic insight
The defensible lane in local discovery isn't out-listing the directories — it's compiling what's actually happening with editorial judgment, then turning it into the user's day. DoStuff proves the curated-not-algorithmic model (and the email-led retention engine) across its markets; it isn't in SB. SB Daymaker owns that vacant territory with a happenings-first cascade plus the personal save/plan layer no calendar offers. Curated personalization (occasion tags) is how you tune it — a strong supporting feature, not the headline.
02 — Product Overview

What SB Daymaker actually is

A daily-ritual mobile web app you open like you check the weather — it tells you what Santa Barbara is about today, tuned to who you are and what the day holds.

The core experience

The ritual

SB Daymaker — the hero

A living golden-hour hero in two layers: a sky strip showing the date and live conditions (weather, surf, sunset) with the sun tracking the real time of day, and a living editorial pick card that surfaces from the mountain silhouette — title, venue, time, and a direct CTA. Context-aware copy ("✨ Tonight's pick," "☁️ Gray day move") reacts to time and weather. Auto-drafted each morning, founder-approved. Never blank.

Supporting feature

Occasion tags (the Lens)

A friendly "what are you up to?" control that filters the app across ten occasion tags: Date Night, Family Day, Nightlife/After Dark, Catch a Show, Arts & Culture, Outdoors & Active, Wine & Food, Free in SB, Hosting Visitors, Solo. The tag sets what; the cascade sets order.

The horizons

Today / This Week / This Month

Three time horizons, each with its own treatment: today's singular ritual, the week's planning rail, the month's big-rocks view for aspirational planning.

The moat

The personal layer

Save anything (on-device, two-state "want to go"/"been," no account), then share — pick one item or a multi-select batch and SB Daymaker generates a real view-only link anyone can open and copy to their own saved list. A passwordless magic-link restores saves on a new device. The Saved list carries a Near Me sort, and One Perfect SB Day seeds it with a ready-made day.

The content pillars

Everything is a "thing" classified by a happening tier that drives the cascade:

  • Tier 1 — discrete dated happenings (this show, festival, gathering). The lead; owns the hero. Sourced from Ticketmaster + venue/civic feeds; ticketing hands off to AXS/TM (SB Daymaker never transacts).
  • Tier 2 — recurring rhythms (Wednesday wine night, First Thursday art walk, the standing market). Predictable reasons to come back, including happy hours.
  • Tier 3 — evergreen, activity-framed reasons-to-go (dog beach, sunset hike, the bakery crawl). The anti-empty floor — the city is never empty; no bare listings, every place serves a reason to go.
  • First Looks + New This Week — new openings and a visible-freshness surface that also feeds the 2×/week email; the weekly-check habit.
  • Curated city guides (Discover SB) — two groups: neighborhood guides (Funk Zone, Downtown & State St., the Waterfront, Montecito, the Mesa) and theme guides (A Perfect Funk Zone Afternoon, Free Santa Barbara, First-Timer's SB, SB with Kids, Rainy Day in SB) — each surfacing the live happenings scoped to it.

The two sides of the product

SB Daymaker is a two-sided build. The consumer app is what the public sees. The admin cockpit is the founder's private dashboard — six surfaces (Daily Approval, Content Review, Staleness board, First Looks composer, Evergreen place manager, Guides editor) that make the whole thing maintainable in minutes a day. The app cannot run without the cockpit; they are co-equal MVP deliverables.

03 — Value Proposition

Why it wins

For the user

A knowing friend, not a database

Existing tools make you scan and decide. SB Daymaker decides with you — curated, opinionated, warm. It removes the work of figuring out what's good.

Made for this moment

Occasion tags + weather-reactivity + time horizons mean the app feels tuned to you, today — a rainy Tuesday with the kids surfaces something completely different from a clear Friday date night.

From discovery to done

Save → share. The Saved list makes it effortless to keep your picks and hand them off: a real view-only link companions can open and copy to their own list. No other SB tool closes the loop from "what's happening" to "it's saved and shared."

Zero friction

No account, no email wall, no login. On-device save, with a passwordless magic-link to restore on a new device — sync without becoming a sign-up gate. Designed for an audience that won't tolerate friction, accessible by default (WCAG 2.2 AA).

05 — Go-to-Market Strategy

How the world finds SB Daymaker

A daily-ritual local product grows through habit and word-of-mouth, not paid acquisition. The strategy is to be so genuinely useful and shareable that locals do the marketing.

The growth engine, in order of leverage

1 · The email edition (the proven retention engine)

In local discovery, the owned email edition is the single biggest driver of recurring use (6AM City: ~1M+ subscribers, ~45–50% open; DoStuff is email-led). SB Daymaker makes it first-class, sending 2×/week — a midweek "what's on / tonight" edition + a weekend digest, covering both what's new and what's coming up — assembled from already-approved content, founder auto-sends with veto. Never a wall. This is the heartbeat that brings residents back.

2 · Shareable artifacts (the organic core)

The daily hero is screenshot-and-send beautiful, and First Looks — "did you know [new spot] just opened?" — is inherently shareable local news. The biggest new lever: finishing a plan produces a real plan view-link anyone can open to see the plan and save their own copy to tweak — delivered via the native share sheet. Trip planning is social (couples, families, residents hosting visitors), so every shared plan is both a trusted impression and a growth channel.

3 · SEO via evergreen guides

The five flagship city guides ("Free Santa Barbara," "First-Timer's SB") target high-intent search queries visitors and locals actually type. Evergreen content compounds — write once, rank for years. This is the inbound discovery channel.

4 · Local seeding & community presence

Seed the app in the places SB locals already gather: neighborhood groups, the farmers market, local subreddits and Facebook groups, library and community boards. A genuinely useful local tool spreads through local networks if introduced authentically.

5 · Business relationships (compounding flywheel)

As First Looks features new openings, businesses notice. Featured businesses share their feature to their audiences — extending reach — and become the foundation of the future operator-photo and sponsorship programs. The content is the business development.

6 · The submission engine (sourcing & growth in one)

Public "submit your event" and "claim your business" forms turn organizers and venues into both the sourcing engine and a growth channel — every organizer who submits has an incentive to share their listing to their own audience. It flips content from "chased" to "comes to you," captures the grassroots/social-first events that have no API, and seeds the operator relationships that later power photos and sponsorship. The official SB tourism site already proves locals will use a submission form.

Positioning & voice

The positioning line: "Find what's worth doing in Santa Barbara today — find it, save it, share it." It names the happenings (what's worth doing), foregrounds the personal layer (save it, share it — the half no competitor offers), and keeps the daily ritual ("today"). The brand voice is the knowing local friend: warm, specific, never breathless, never corporate. The "we can't be bought" trust promise is stated publicly.

What we deliberately don't do

  • No paid ads at launch — wrong economics for a hobby-scale daily app; word-of-mouth is the channel.
  • No multi-city expansion — depth in one place is the entire edge.
  • No email-list building gate — email is first-class and 2×/week, but always a single opt-in field, never a wall.

The three cost safeguards (in the build)

  1. Lazy-load every image — Google photo requests fire only when an image scrolls into view. This is the single biggest lever; without it, costs run 4–5× higher.
  2. Hard billing cap + alert — a Google Cloud spend ceiling (start ~$50/mo) that emails before anything runs away. Eliminates tail risk.
  3. Photo resolution order — ambient → Pexels (free); business → Google. Pre-built to insert owned/operator photos ahead of Google later with no rearchitecting.
The bootstrap logic
Start 100% on Google because it costs zero founder time today. It's effectively free while small. The operator-photo migration activates exactly when it should — when cost becomes worth addressing and traffic gives leverage with businesses. Google carries the proving phase for free; operator photos take over when scale makes cost matter.
08 — Monetization Paths

How it earns (when you want it to)

SB Daymaker can remain a free hobby indefinitely. But the architecture supports several local-first revenue paths that align with — rather than corrupt — the curated experience.

Path A · Highest fit

Featured listings & sponsored placements

Businesses pay for premium placement or a "featured" badge — clearly labeled, never deceptive. Natural fit with First Looks and the place directory. Aligns incentives: the businesses you cover want visibility.

Path B · Natural extension

Local sponsorship of the daily/weekend digest

"This weekend, brought to you by [local business]." The shareable artifacts become sponsorship inventory once they have reach. Tasteful, single-sponsor, local-only.

Path C · Relationship-based

Business profiles / enhanced pages

A modest recurring fee for businesses to maintain an enhanced profile (their photos, their happy hour, their events). Pairs perfectly with the future operator-photo program.

Path D · Later / optional

Affiliate & events giveaways

Ticketing affiliate revenue on the AXS/TM hand-off (where programs allow), and sponsored ticket giveaways — both deferred, both requiring partner relationships.

The guardrail
Monetization must never make the Lens surface a worse pick. Sponsored content is always labeled, always quality-gated, and never displaces the genuinely-best recommendation. The trust is the product; protect it ruthlessly.

What we explicitly rule out

  • In-app ticketing / payment — we hand off to AXS/TM by design (legal, scope, trust).
  • Display-ad networks — cheapens the editorial feel, wrong for the brand.
  • Selling user data — there's barely any (on-device, no accounts), and it would betray the trust model.

Santa Barbara County is home to roughly 443,000 residents, and the South Coast draws about 6.5 million visitors a year — of whom 36% stay overnight (~2.3M) and 80% say they'll return. Against a combined local-plus-visitor base of that size, even 10,000 weekly active users is low-single-digit penetration of the genuinely addressable audience. The higher tiers below are therefore framed conservatively — they represent a modest, believable share of the market, not saturation.

Why the visitor base matters so much here
A daily "what's worth doing today" app is exactly what an overnight visitor wants — and SB refreshes ~2.3M overnight visitors a year, each needing 1–3 days of recommendations. This is a constantly-replenishing audience on top of the resident base, which is unusual and favorable for a local-discovery product. It also means visitor-driven usage is seasonal and spiky, which the tiers treat as average weekly figures.

10 — Risks & Considerations

Eyes open

⚠️ Content volume on quiet days

Mitigated by design. The happenings-first cascade keeps a Tier-3 evergreen floor — SB Daymaker is never empty because the city itself (dog beach, the market, a sunset hike) is always a reason to go. Evergreen places + guides carry the quiet Tuesdays; the wide definition of "happening" + lead-and-blend keep the screen full.

⚠️ Control overload / complexity

Mitigated. A single compact dual-button row (Lens + Refine) replaces the old tall pill + chip stack — all filter complexity lives inside bottom sheets, invisible until needed. First-run is a gentle overlay banner asking "What vibe are you looking for?" — the app is usable behind it immediately, no blocking gate. Designed for an older, simplicity-seeking audience.

⚠️ Founder time / sustainability

Mitigated. AI-drafts-you-approve + auto-publish-with-veto means the app runs even on busy days; the admin cockpit keeps daily upkeep to ~15 minutes.

⚠️ Data freshness & trust

Mitigated. Happy-hour "last confirmed" staleness model + persona-tag confidence threshold + negative-rule safety (e.g. a 21+ venue can never be tagged Family Day).

⚠️ Legal / data sourcing

Addressed. Licensed APIs (Ticketmaster, Google Places), venue-site facts not AXS scraping, operator-submitted happy hours, original AI-written copy (no reproduced descriptions). A pre-revenue IP/tech attorney review is recommended insurance.

⚠️ Cost scaling with users

Mitigated. Lazy-loading + billing cap keep Google photo costs bounded; the operator-photo program bends the curve down exactly when scale makes it matter.

The honest watch-items

  • DoStuff entering SB — unlikely (market too small) but the one competitor who could replicate the model. Watch.
  • The Independent — most likely local competitor-or-partner; has the calendar, audience, and brand. Keep in view as both.
  • Single-device save (no sync) — an accepted launch tradeoff; the eventual reason to add accounts.
  • Google API terms drift — pricing/caps changed in 2025; verify current terms at billing setup and monitor.
