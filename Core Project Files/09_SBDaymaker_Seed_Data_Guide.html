The one rule that matters most

Claude writes the voice. You (and your sources) supply the facts. If you ask Claude to "generate 150 Santa Barbara restaurants," it will hand you a beautiful list full of plausible, confident, wrong places — closed spots, invented addresses, made-up happy-hour times. You won't notice until a user drives to a restaurant that doesn't exist.

For a product whose entire moat is trust and a knowing-local voice, hallucinated facts are poison. So everywhere in this guide, Claude's job is to transform real facts you give it — write the blurb, pick the occasion tags, classify the happening tier, draft the local's-secret note — and never to be the source of a name, address, date, time, or price.

00 — Orientation

What "seed data" means, and the two stages

"Seed data" is the starting content that makes the app real — the places, events, happy hours, and guides that fill the three sections (Explore · Saved · Discover SB). You'll build it in two very different stages with two very different quality bars.

StageWhenWhat it isCan Claude make it up?
1 · Dev fixturesNow — for build Phase 1~20–30 throwaway rows so every screen has something to show while you build.Yes — it's scaffolding Nobody sees it; invented content is fine here.
2 · Launch contentBefore launch, then foreverThe real ~100–150 things, sourced and verified, then Claude-enriched and approved by you.No — real users see this Facts must be real and checked.
Why this guide is standalone

You'll run Stage 2 every time you add content — not once. So it lives in its own guide, not buried in the one-time build plan. The build plan (Document 8) just points here at Phase 1 ("load the dev fixtures") and again near launch ("run the launch-content workflow").

01 — The field-source map

Where every fact is allowed to come from

This is the most important table in the guide. For each kind of field, it says who the source of truth is. Tape it to the wall. Green = you or a real source. Blue = Claude proposes, you confirm. Red = Claude must never originate it.

Field on a "thing"Source of truthHow
Name, address, lat/long, phone, website, hours, photosnever ClaudeGoogle Places, the venue's own site, or your own verification. These are the facts a user acts on.
Event dates & times; happy-hour windows; recurring schedulesnever ClaudeThe official listing or the venue. A wrong time sends someone to a locked door — the worst failure mode.
Type (place / event / happy hour); neighborhood; nearby_zoneClaude proposes → you confirmDerivable from the real address. Claude can suggest; you sanity-check the zone.
happening_tier & happening_categoryrule + Claude proposesTier is derived by rule (has-date → 1, recurring → 2, else → 3). Claude proposes the category from the real facts.
Blurb · reason_to_go · the local's-secret note · occasion tagsClaude drafts → you approveThis is the voice work — exactly what Claude is for. Always from facts you supplied, never invented detail.

Read the table as one sentence: the closer a field is to something a user physically acts on, the further Claude stays from originating it.

Stage 1 · do this now

Dev fixtures — make the screens light up

Goal: ~20–30 rows of throwaway content so that when you build a screen in Phase 1, it has something to render. This is the one place you can let Claude generate freely — it's scaffolding, not shown to anyone. You'll delete it before launch.

1

Gather a few real anchors (5 minutes)

From memory, jot down 6–10 Santa Barbara places you personally know are real — a couple in each zone (Funk Zone, Downtown/State, Waterfront, Montecito, Mesa). Just names. These keep the fixture data feeling like SB instead of generic filler, and they give you something recognizable to eyeball on screen.

✓ Check before moving on
  • You have at least 6 real place names written down.
  • At least 3 different neighborhoods are represented.

2

Ask Claude for fixture rows in the right shape

Open a normal Claude chat. Attach (or paste) sbdaymaker_schema.sql so Claude matches your real columns and enum values, then paste the prompt below. The goal is coverage of variety — at least one of every type, every zone, a few events dated relative to today, a couple of happy hours, and a guide or two — so every screen and empty-state has something to test.

Copy-paste prompt — fixture generationI've attached sbdaymaker_schema.sql, the database schema for my Santa Barbara discovery app. Generate ~25 fixture rows as valid PostgreSQL INSERT statements that match this schema exactly (correct table names, columns, and enum values). This is THROWAWAY dev data to make my screens render — it does not need to be real, but it must be realistic and varied. Cover: - every `type` value (place, event, happy hour, first look) - all neighborhood / nearby_zone values, including goleta - 4–5 events with dates spread across the next 2 weeks (use dates relative to today: 2026-06-21) - 2–3 happy_hour_windows and 1–2 recurring_schedules - 2 guides: one neighborhood guide (with a zone) and one theme guide (with a tag) - a short blurb and a reason_to_go for each place, in a warm, knowing-local voice Use these REAL anchor places where they fit, and clearly make the rest obviously-placeholder names (e.g. "Test Cafe — Funk Zone"): [paste your 6–10 real names here] Return only the SQL, ready to run. Add a comment at the top: "-- DEV FIXTURES — delete before launch".
✓ Check before moving on
  • The output is pure SQL with the "delete before launch" comment at the top.
  • You can see at least one row per type, and a goleta row.
  • Event dates are in the future, not the past.
  • The two guides include one with a zone and one with a tag (the v9 guide kinds).

3

Load it into your local database

Save Claude's output as seed_fixtures.sql. In your project (with Supabase running locally), run it against your database — your build tool (Claude Code) can do this for you: just ask it to "run seed_fixtures.sql against the local Supabase database." If anything errors, paste the exact error back to Claude and ask it to fix the SQL — usually it's a mismatched enum value.

✓ Check before moving on
  • The script runs with zero errors.
  • Querying the things table returns ~25 rows.
  • No red error text mentioning an invalid enum or missing column.

4

Eyeball it in the app

Once Phase 1 screens exist, open the app and walk the three sections. The fixtures exist to prove the plumbing works — that data flows from the database to the screen — not to look perfect.

✓ Stage 1 is done when
  • Explore shows a hero + a feed of cards.
  • Saved shows items after you tap save (and the "been" flip works).
  • Discover SB shows your two guides, and opening one shows its scoped happenings.
  • Near Me re-sorts the list when toggled.
  • Empty states appear where you'd expect (e.g. Saved before you save anything).
Stage 2 · before launch, then ongoing

Launch content — the real Santa Barbara

Goal: the real ~100–150 things users will actually trust. This is four passes — source → enrich → approve → load. Doing it by hand here is not busywork: it is the exact workflow your nightly automated pipeline (build Phase 8) will later run for you. So this stage doubles as that pipeline's blueprint.

A

Source the facts — real, by hand (the slow, important part)

Build a working spreadsheet of the things you want to launch with. One row per place/event. Fill the never-Claude columns from real sources: Google Places / Google Maps (name, address, lat/long, hours, phone, website, photo reference), the venue's own site or the official event listing (event dates, happy-hour times), and your own SB knowledge for the obvious zone. This pass is you + real sources only. Leave the voice columns (blurb, tags, note) blank — that's Claude's job next.

Tip

Aim for a quality bar, not a quantity bar: ~30 genuinely good, verified things beat 150 shaky ones. The evergreen Tier-3 floor (Document 7, moat 3) means the app won't feel empty at 30.

✓ Check before moving on
  • Every row has a real, complete address you could navigate to.
  • Every event/happy-hour row has a date/time copied from an official source.
  • The voice columns are intentionally blank.

B

Let Claude enrich — in batches, with the guardrail baked in

Now hand Claude the real rows and ask only for the voice + classification. Work in batches of ~10–15 so you can review carefully. The prompt below contains the anti-hallucination instruction every batch must include — it tells Claude to use only your facts and to flag anything it can't fill rather than invent it.

Copy-paste prompt — enrichment batchBelow is a batch of REAL Santa Barbara places/events I have already verified. Each row's facts (name, address, hours, dates, times) are correct and fixed. Your job is ONLY to add the editorial fields for each row: - blurb: 1–2 sentences, warm "knowing local friend" voice - reason_to_go: one short phrase — why today - happening_category: choose from the schema's allowed values - occasion_tags: pick 1–3 from {Date Night, Family Day, Nightlife, Catch a Show, Arts & Culture, Outdoors & Active, Wine & Food, Free in SB, Hosting Visitors, Solo} - local_note: one optional insider line, ONLY if you genuinely know something true about this specific place; otherwise leave blank CRITICAL RULES: - Use ONLY the facts I provide. Do NOT add, infer, or invent any place, address, date, time, price, or factual claim. - If you are not sure about a detail, leave that field blank and add it to a "FLAGGED" list at the end. Never guess. - Do not write anything that asserts a fact I didn't give you. Return a table with my original rows plus the new columns, then the FLAGGED list. ROWS: [paste your 10–15 verified rows here]
✓ Check before moving on
  • Claude added only voice/classification columns — it did not change any address, date, or time.
  • You read the FLAGGED list and resolved each item yourself.
  • No local_note asserts something you can't personally verify.

C

You approve — the human gate

Read every blurb. This is the curation moat (Document 7) in action — your voice, your standards. Fix anything that sounds generic or "AI." Then do the fact spot-check: this is the step that protects users.

✓ Check before moving on
  • You re-read every blurb and it sounds like you, not a template.
  • Address spot-check: pick 5 rows at random and confirm each address in Google Maps.
  • Time spot-check: pick 3 happy-hour/event rows and confirm the times against the official source.
  • Zero past dates. Zero "permanently closed" places (re-check anything you're unsure of).

D

Format, load, and validate

Ask Claude to turn your approved spreadsheet into INSERT statements against sbdaymaker_schema.sql (same as the fixture step, but now with your real, approved data). Load it, then run the final validation. Keep your spreadsheet — it's your content master and the input your Phase-8 pipeline will eventually read automatically.

✓ Stage 2 batch is done when
  • The INSERTs load with zero errors.
  • The real rows render across Explore / Saved / Discover SB.
  • Trust test: a sponsored item and a non-sponsored item appear in ranked order with no special treatment — the ranker never reads sponsor status (canon rule).
  • You've deleted the Stage-1 dev fixtures so they don't mix with real content.
02 — The guardrail, in five lines

Pin this above your desk

  1. Facts never come from Claude — names, addresses, hours, dates, times, prices, phone numbers.
  2. Claude only writes voice and classification — blurb, reason-to-go, tags, tier/category, local note.
  3. Every enrichment prompt says: "Use only the facts I provide; do not invent; flag what you can't fill."
  4. You spot-check every batch — 5 addresses, 3 times, in the real source.
  5. When in doubt, leave it blank. An empty field is fine; a confident wrong fact is not.
03 — Cost & the pipeline tie-in

What this costs, and where it goes next

The enrichment is cheap, and the hand-run workflow becomes the automation.

  • Cost. Enrichment runs through the Claude API on the cheapest tier (Haiku) in batch mode — fractions of a cent per row. It sits comfortably inside the ~$45/mo platform floor. The metered cost to watch is photos (Google Places), not text — which is why operator-supplied photos are the long-term cost lever (canon).
  • The pipeline tie-in. Stage 2's four passes — source → enrich → approve → load — are exactly what the nightly automated pipeline (build Phase 8) will do on its own: pull facts from sources, batch-enrich with Claude, surface for your one-tap approval in the cockpit, then publish. Running it by hand now means that when you build Phase 8, you're automating a process you already understand and trust.
The mindset to keep

You are not asking Claude to know Santa Barbara. You're asking it to describe a Santa Barbara you've already verified. Keep that line clean and your content stays trustworthy — which is the whole product.
